Theory of Chladni Plates

What is this simulation?

Chladni patterns occur when a thin metal plate is subjected to transverse standing waves induced by an acoustic tone. The plate does not vibrate uniformly: it forms regions of maximum vibration (antinodes) and regions of absolute rest called nodal lines.

Physical Behavior & The Wave Equation

Each grain of sand experiences physical acceleration on the vibrating surface. In active antinode zones, the sand is catapulted into the air in a random walk. Once particles fall onto a nodal line, they stop bouncing and accumulate, revealing the invisible geometry of sound.

For an ideal square plate, the standing wave pattern Z(x, y) is approximated by:

Z(x, y) = cos(nπx) · cos(mπy) + K · cos(mπx) · cos(nπy)

The coefficients n and m represent the harmonic wave modes, and K is a boundary constant that determines how the plate is excited or bowed.

How to use the Simulator:

  • Wave Modes: Tweak the n and m sliders to increase the frequency and observe different geometries.
  • The K Slider: Set K to -1.0 for diagonal and orthogonal lines, or slide toward +1.0 or +2.0 to reveal concentric rings.
  • Motion Trails: Toggle "Trails" to visualize the pathway particles take to reach the quiet nodal lines.
  • 3D Analysis: Switch to "3D View" and drag the screen to rotate.
